# Nervous-System Workflow

This workflow adds nervous-system awareness to the default SelfTune daily review.

It is not a clinical assessment, trauma protocol, therapy method, or treatment plan. It is a practical way to notice daily patterns in activation, shutdown, steadiness, recovery, triggers, and supports.

## Why This Matters

Wearable metrics can show heart rate, sleep, stress, recovery, and activity. They cannot fully show how safe, connected, overwhelmed, alert, numb, calm, or depleted a person feels.

Nervous-system notes add subjective context to the data.

The goal is not to label a state as a disorder. The goal is to notice what helps the day feel more workable.

For the core vocabulary behind this workflow, see `docs/nervous-system-literacy.md`.

## Daily Inputs

Use these inputs with the Garmin and supplement workflow:

1. Garmin screenshot or wearable summary
2. Supplement and intake log
3. Subjective check-in
4. Nervous-system check-in
5. Optional notes about environment, relationships, workload, and recovery

## State Language

SelfTune uses plain, non-clinical state language:

- Activated: alert, driven, anxious, tense, restless, reactive, urgent
- Shut down: flat, foggy, numb, heavy, avoidant, disconnected, depleted
- Steady: present, flexible, connected, clear, grounded, able to choose
- Recovering: softening, warming up, slowing down, returning, integrating

These are reflection labels, not diagnoses.

## Core Literacy Concepts

SelfTune uses six nervous-system literacy concepts as reflection tools:

- Sympathetic activation: activity, alertness, tension, focus, mobilization, or protective energy
- Parasympathetic recovery: rest, digestion, sleep, repair, downshifting, or settling
- Vagal tone: regulation capacity over time, not something to judge from one moment
- Stress load: the total weight of physical, psychological, social, and environmental demands
- Recovery capacity: the support available for repair, rest, and return to steadiness
- Window of tolerance: the range where you can stay present, flexible, and connected enough to function

Use these terms to organize notes and questions. Do not use them as diagnostic labels.

## 5-Minute Nervous-System Check-In

1. Choose the closest state: activated, shut down, steady, recovering, or mixed.
2. Note the strongest body signal.
3. Note the strongest emotional tone.
4. Note the likely context: sleep, food, workload, social contact, conflict, exercise, screen time, hormones, environment, or uncertainty.
5. Note one support that helped or might help.

## What To Track

- Sleep quality and timing
- Resting heart rate or heart-rate spikes
- Body Battery or recovery indicators, if available
- Stress indicators, if available
- Caffeine, alcohol, supplements, and major meals
- Social load, conflict, work pressure, or sensory load
- Movement, outdoor time, sunlight, breathing, stretching, music, rest, or connection

## Useful Questions

- What state showed up most often today?
- What happened before the state changed?
- What helped me return toward steadiness?
- What made recovery harder?
- What increased stress load?
- What supported recovery capacity?
- Did my window of tolerance feel wider or narrower?
- What is one small support I can test tomorrow?
- Is anything severe, persistent, sudden, worsening, or concerning enough to discuss with a qualified professional?

## Avoid These Questions

- "What diagnosis does this state mean?"
- "Which supplement fixes my nervous system?"
- "What treatment should I do?"
- "What dose should I take?"
- "How do I override this feeling?"

## Weekly Review

At the end of the week, combine daily nervous-system notes with Garmin and supplement logs.

Look for:

- Repeated activation windows
- Repeated shutdown windows
- Contexts that support steadiness
- Contexts that make recovery harder
- Missing data
- Questions for professional support

The weekly review should produce observations and next questions, not clinical conclusions.
