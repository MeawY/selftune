# Garmin and Nervous-System Integration

This guide explains how to use Garmin metrics as context for nervous-system reflection in SelfTune.

Garmin data should be treated as a hint, not a diagnosis. A low score, high stress reading, or unusual HRV value does not mean something is wrong with you. It means there may be useful context to observe.

## Relevant Garmin Metrics

### HRV

Heart rate variability, or HRV, describes subtle variation in time between heartbeats.

In SelfTune, HRV can be used as one possible context marker for recovery and adaptability. It should not be used to diagnose nervous-system health, identify a cause, or judge a single day as good or bad.

Can help with:

- Noticing recovery trends over time
- Comparing subjective refreshment with wearable recovery signals
- Asking better questions about sleep, stress load, and recovery capacity

Cannot tell you:

- The exact psychological, dietary, supplement, or environmental trigger behind a change
- Whether you have a medical condition
- What treatment, supplement, or dosage you need

### Stress Level

Garmin stress metrics may provide context for physiological arousal.

In SelfTune, high stress readings are not treated as bad grades. They may reflect exercise, excitement, focus, illness, caffeine, heat, conflict, overwork, poor sleep, or many other demands.

Can help with:

- Finding periods worth reviewing
- Comparing high-stress windows with breathing, pacing, tension, and emotional tone
- Noticing whether stress load stayed high for long periods

Cannot tell you:

- Whether stress was good, bad, emotional, physical, or environmental
- Whether you were anxious
- Whether the body response was inappropriate

### Sleep Score and Sleep Data

Sleep data can provide useful context for recovery capacity.

In SelfTune, sleep metrics should be paired with subjective refreshment. A score alone does not capture how clear, steady, rested, or emotionally resourced you felt after waking.

Can help with:

- Comparing sleep timing with next-day energy
- Noticing whether recovery capacity may have been lower
- Reviewing evening routines and screen load

Cannot tell you:

- Exactly why sleep was poor
- Whether a single night explains the whole next day
- Whether a sleep issue is medical

### Resting Heart Rate

Resting heart rate can provide context for baseline load, recovery, exertion, and stress.

In SelfTune, it should be reviewed as a pattern over time and alongside context such as illness, sleep, hydration, alcohol, caffeine, exercise, and emotional load.

Can help with:

- Noticing changes from your own baseline
- Adding context to fatigue, activation, or recovery notes
- Reviewing exertion and recovery patterns

Cannot tell you:

- Whether you have a cardiovascular or autonomic condition
- The exact reason for a change
- What intervention is needed

### Body Battery

Body Battery can be treated as a broad estimate of energy and recovery context.

In SelfTune, it is most useful when compared with subjective experience: Did you feel depleted, steady, wired, foggy, or capable?

Can help with:

- Comparing perceived energy with wearable estimates
- Finding days when recovery capacity seemed lower
- Reviewing whether rest, food, movement, and wind-down helped

Cannot tell you:

- Your actual worth, discipline, or health status
- Whether you should push or rest without considering context
- A medical explanation for fatigue

## Combining Garmin Data With Subjective Notes

Use Garmin as a prompt to check in with the body.

### Gut Context

If Garmin shows poor recovery or sustained stress, cross-reference digestion:

- Regular
- Sluggish
- Bloated
- Unsettled
- Constipated
- Diarrhea
- Appetite changes

Do not treat gut symptoms as proof of a nervous-system state. Use them as context.

### Physical Tension

When stress readings are high, check:

- Breath quality
- Jaw, neck, and shoulder tension
- Headache
- Cold hands or feet
- Restlessness
- Stillness or freeze-like heaviness

### Emotional Tone

Compare low Body Battery, low recovery, or high stress windows with:

- Rushing
- Irritability
- Vague anxiety
- Focused excitement
- Numbness
- Calm
- Feeling overloaded

The same metric can pair with different emotional states on different days.

## Daily Reflection Questions

- When did Garmin stress peak, and what was happening?
- Did that period feel like pressure, excitement, focus, exercise, conflict, heat, caffeine, or something else?
- Was my breathing shallow, easy, rapid, slow, or mixed?
- Did I feel rushed, pressured, or short on time?
- How was digestion today?
- Did I consciously slow down movement, speech, or pacing?
- What helped the brake come online?
- What helped recovery capacity?

## Weekly Pattern Review

At the end of the week, look for patterns rather than single-day conclusions.

Useful questions:

- Did HRV, sleep score, or subjective refreshment improve on lower-pressure days?
- Which evening habits seemed to support better sleep or recovery?
- Did sustained high stress readings appear alongside digestive discomfort, shallow breathing, or tension?
- Did morning light, offline time, warm baths, slower pacing, or longer exhales seem to support wind-down?
- Did I become more anxious because of the data itself?

## Avoiding Data Stress

Wearable data can become stressful when numbers start feeling like grades.

SelfTune frames the numbers differently:

- A high stress score is not failure.
- A low Body Battery is not brokenness.
- A low HRV day is not proof that something is wrong.
- A poor sleep score is a reason to observe, not a reason to panic.

Try using the data as a gentle signal:

"My body may be working hard today. What would help me reduce load or support recovery?"

## Safety Boundaries

Garmin data is not a medical diagnosis. SelfTune cannot diagnose, treat, or cure autonomic dysfunction, cardiovascular conditions, sleep disorders, depression, anxiety, or any other condition.

Consider qualified professional support if symptoms are severe, sudden, persistent, worsening, disruptive, or concerning, especially intense palpitations, chronic insomnia, severe dizziness, fainting, chest pain, breathing difficulty, or anything that feels unsafe.
