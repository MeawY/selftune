# Nervous-System Literacy

Nervous-system literacy is the ability to notice and describe your state without turning it into a diagnosis.

In SelfTune, nervous-system literacy helps connect three kinds of information:

1. Wearable data, such as Garmin sleep, heart rate, stress, activity, and recovery metrics
2. Context, such as supplements, food, caffeine, workload, relationships, and environment
3. Subjective experience, such as tension, fatigue, recovery, steadiness, and felt safety

These concepts are used for self-reflection only. They are not clinical categories, diagnostic labels, or treatment instructions.

## Core Concepts

### Sympathetic Activation

Sympathetic activation is an activity, alertness, and mobilization mode.

In daily notes, it may show up as:

- Tension
- Urgency
- Focus
- Restlessness
- Alertness
- Defensive energy
- Higher heart rate
- Difficulty slowing down

SelfTune use:

Track when activation appears, what came before it, what it helped you do, and what helped you come back toward steadiness.

### Parasympathetic Recovery

Parasympathetic recovery refers to rest, digestion, sleep, repair, and downshifting.

In daily notes, it may show up as:

- Easier breathing
- Sleepiness
- Digestive settling
- Warmth
- Slower pace
- Lower urgency
- Better sleep preparation
- A sense of being able to pause

SelfTune use:

Track what supports recovery, such as sleep timing, meals, quiet time, safe connection, gentle movement, or reduced stimulation.

### Vagal Tone

Vagal tone is a general way to talk about the body's ability to regulate between activation and recovery.

In SelfTune, this term should be used carefully. It is not something to diagnose from a single feeling, screenshot, or wearable metric.

In daily notes, regulation capacity may show up as:

- Returning to calm after stress
- More flexible breathing
- Better digestion under ordinary conditions
- Feeling socially connected
- Feeling able to choose instead of react
- Recovering after activity or conflict

SelfTune use:

Track signs of regulation over time instead of trying to judge vagal tone from one day.

### Stress Load

Stress load is the total weight of psychological, physical, social, and environmental demands.

It may include:

- Poor sleep
- High workload
- Conflict
- Time pressure
- Intense exercise
- Illness or discomfort
- Sensory load
- Caffeine or alcohol
- Under-eating or delayed meals
- Too many changes at once

SelfTune use:

Track cumulative load. A difficult day may not come from one cause; it may come from several small loads stacking together.

### Recovery Capacity

Recovery capacity is the amount of support available for repair, rest, and return to steadiness.

It may be supported by:

- Sleep
- Food and hydration
- Rest
- Felt safety
- Social support
- Gentle movement
- Time outside
- Predictable routines
- Lower stimulation
- Adequate recovery after exertion

SelfTune use:

Track whether your recovery capacity seems high, medium, or low before interpreting stress, supplements, or wearable data.

### Window of Tolerance

The window of tolerance is a practical reflection concept for the range where you can stay present, flexible, and connected enough to function.

Outside that range, you may notice more activation or more shutdown.

In daily notes, a wider window may feel like:

- More choice
- More patience
- More stable focus
- Easier recovery after stress
- Better ability to communicate
- Less urgency or collapse

A narrower window may feel like:

- Faster overwhelm
- Irritability
- Foggy shutdown
- Avoidance
- Feeling easily pushed past capacity
- Needing more time to recover

SelfTune use:

Track what widens or narrows your window across days. Use this as reflection language, not as a clinical conclusion.

## How To Use These Concepts With Garmin

Garmin can add useful context, especially when looking at patterns over time.

Examples:

- Sleep score and sleep duration can give context for recovery capacity.
- Heart-rate spikes can give context for activation, exercise, stress, caffeine, or other demands.
- Body Battery can give context for perceived energy and recovery, if available.
- Stress metrics can be useful, but should not override subjective experience.

Do not treat Garmin data as proof of a nervous-system state. Treat it as one layer of context.

## How To Use These Concepts With Supplements

Supplement notes can add context, but they should not be used to claim cause and effect from a single day.

Useful supplement context:

- Name
- Amount, if known
- Time taken
- Taken with or without food
- Reason for taking it
- Other changes that happened the same day
- Subjective notes before and after

Avoid using SelfTune to decide dosage, safety, combinations, medication changes, or treatment plans. Bring those questions to a qualified professional.

## Daily Reflection Questions

- What state was most present today?
- Did the day feel more activated, shut down, steady, recovering, or mixed?
- What seemed to increase stress load?
- What seemed to support recovery capacity?
- Did my window of tolerance feel wider or narrower?
- What did Garmin add as context?
- What did supplement notes add as context?
- What is one small observation I can continue tomorrow?

## Safety Boundaries

These concepts can be useful for reflection, but they can also become too certain if used carelessly.

Do not use them to:

- Diagnose yourself or another person
- Explain every symptom
- Ignore severe or persistent distress
- Make supplement, medication, or treatment decisions
- Replace qualified professional support

If symptoms are severe, sudden, persistent, worsening, or concerning, consider seeking qualified professional support.
